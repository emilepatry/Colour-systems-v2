# Communication & Ethics Framework
*Purpose: Frameworks for stakeholder communication, presenting design work, and ethical design checks.*

A framework for presenting design decisions, gathering feedback, and ensuring ethical design practices.

---

## Design Principle: Compassion Over Correction

Design for the failure state as a moment of support. Avoid guilt, judgment, or punishment—the patient should never fear opening the app after a hard week.

This principle applies to both:
- **Product design** — How we handle user failures
- **Team communication** — How we receive and give feedback

---

## Part 1: Communicating Design Decisions

### The Challenge

Design decisions often need to be explained to stakeholders who:
- Have different mental models than designers
- Focus on different success metrics
- Need to understand trade-offs quickly
- May not see the user research behind decisions

### The 3-Step Feedback Process

**1. Frame the Problem First**

Before showing solutions, ensure everyone understands:
- What user problem are we solving?
- What evidence do we have? (research, data, stories)
- What constraints are we working within?

**2. Present Decisions as Hypotheses**

Position designs as testable hypotheses, not final answers:
- "We believe [change] will improve [metric] because [insight]"
- "If this works, we expect to see [outcome]"
- "We'll validate by [measurement approach]"

**3. Invite Specific Feedback**

Guide feedback toward useful input:
- "What concerns do you have about [specific aspect]?"
- "Does this align with what you're hearing from [stakeholder group]?"
- "What would make you more confident in this direction?"

### Meeting Preparation Checklist

- [ ] Clear problem statement ready
- [ ] User evidence summarized (quotes, data, stories)
- [ ] Key trade-offs identified
- [ ] Success metrics defined
- [ ] Specific questions for stakeholders prepared
- [ ] Next steps outlined

## Part 2: Ethical Design

### Why Ethics Matters in Product Design

Behavioral design is powerful. The same principles that help users can also harm them if misapplied. Our responsibility is to use psychology to serve user goals, not exploit user vulnerabilities.

### The 3 Ethics Tests

#### 1. The Regret Test

> "If users knew what we know, would they take the same action?"

Ask yourself:
- Does the user have all the information they need?
- Are we hiding anything that would change their decision?
- Would they feel tricked later?

**Fail examples:**
- Hidden fees revealed at checkout
- Subscription sign-ups buried in terms
- Dark patterns that exploit inattention

#### 2. The Real-Life Test

> "If this was a physical store, would this interaction feel appropriate?"

Ask yourself:
- Would a human say this to a customer face-to-face?
- Would this feel pushy, manipulative, or uncomfortable in person?
- Does this respect the user's time and attention?

**Fail examples:**
- Constant interruptions (like a salesperson following you around)
- Making it hard to leave or unsubscribe
- Guilt-tripping language

#### 3. The Black Mirror Test

> "Does this design push humanity forward?"

Ask yourself:
- If this design became widespread, would the world be better or worse?
- Are we contributing to healthy digital habits?
- What are the second-order effects?

**Fail examples:**
- Infinite scroll designed to maximize time-on-site
- Notifications designed to create anxiety
- Features that exploit loneliness or FOMO

## Ethical vs. Manipulative Design

### ✅ Ethical Influence

- Helps users achieve **their** goals
- Provides genuine value
- Respects user autonomy
- Transparent about intentions
- Easy to opt-out

### ❌ Manipulation

- Serves only business goals
- Tricks or deceives users
- Exploits psychological weaknesses
- Hidden or deceptive practices
- Difficult to opt-out

---

## Ethical Guidelines for Behavioral Design

### Never Do

1. **Never fake scarcity or urgency** — "Only 2 left!" when there are 2,000
2. **Don't exploit loss aversion for harm** — Creating unnecessary anxiety
3. **Don't make opt-outs hard** — Unsubscribe should be as easy as subscribe
4. **Never use guilt or shame** — No "You haven't logged in for 5 days"
5. **Don't hide important information** — Fees, terms, consequences

### Always Do

1. **Lead with user benefit** — Why should they care?
2. **Be honest about trade-offs** — What are they giving up?
3. **Respect attention** — Don't interrupt without value
4. **Enable control** — Let users customize their experience
5. **Test for unintended harm** — What could go wrong?

## Applying Ethics to Fullscript

### Patient-First Principle

> "Patient trust and wellbeing come before short-term conversion."

This means:
- We don't use urgency/scarcity tactics for refills
- We don't guilt patients about missed doses
- We don't hide practitioner attribution
- We make it easy to pause, cancel, or change plans

### Practitioner Relationship

We support the provider-patient relationship; we never replace it:
- Clinical guidance comes from providers, not our app
- We attribute recommendations to practitioners
- We encourage users to "talk to your care team"

## Common Ethical Dilemmas

### Dilemma: Increasing Refill Rate

**Manipulative approach:** Create anxiety about running out, use countdown timers, make it hard to skip refills

**Ethical approach:** Show when refills are due, explain why consistency matters, make it easy to adjust timing

### Dilemma: Increasing Engagement

**Manipulative approach:** Send frequent notifications, create FOMO, gamify everything

**Ethical approach:** Notify only when genuinely valuable, respect preferences, make content worth returning for

### Dilemma: Collecting Health Data

**Manipulative approach:** Require data entry, hide how data is used, sell data to third parties

**Ethical approach:** Make data entry optional, explain benefits clearly, keep data private and user-controlled

## Quick Reference Checklists

### Before Launching a Feature

- [ ] Passes the Regret Test?
- [ ] Passes the Real-Life Test?
- [ ] Passes the Black Mirror Test?
- [ ] User benefit is clear?
- [ ] Opt-out is easy?
- [ ] No dark patterns?

### Presenting to Stakeholders

- [ ] Problem framed before solution?
- [ ] Evidence summarized?
- [ ] Trade-offs acknowledged?
- [ ] Positioned as hypothesis?
- [ ] Specific feedback requested?
- [ ] Next steps clear?

### Receiving Feedback

- [ ] Listen fully before responding?
- [ ] Understand the underlying concern?
- [ ] Separate personal from professional?
- [ ] Look for the insight in the critique?
- [ ] Follow up with clarifying questions?
